Master Thesis — Team Worm allows O. algarvensis to thrive in nitrogen-limited environments

MPI-MM · Department of Symbiosis · Supervisors: Dr. Caroline Zeidler, Alba Porta Fidalgo · 1st Reviewer: Prof. Dr. Nicole Dubilier
Studied how the gutless oligochaete Olavius algarvensis thrives in nitrogen-poor sediments at Sant'Andrea, Elba — a worm with no digestive system and no nephridia (the organ animals normally use to excrete ammonium), sustained entirely by an obligate symbiosis with at least five bacterial endosymbionts. Found the ammonium transporter Rhp1 upregulated in the trunk segments where the symbionts reside, and localised symbiont glutamine synthetase (GS) within the symbiont layer via HCR-FISH. The co-localisation suggests host-derived ammonium is recycled by the symbionts — effectively replacing the missing nephridia and conserving nitrogen within the partnership. Most prior work on this system focused on carbon transfer; this thesis makes the case for nitrogen as the other half of the story.
MetagenomicsMetatranscriptomicsPhylogeneticsAlphaFoldHMMHCR-FISH

"A gutless worm with no excretory organ, yet thriving in nitrogen-starved sediment. Its five bacterial partners don't just feed it — they recycle the ammonium it produces, taking over the role of nephridia and closing the nitrogen loop within the symbiosis."

Lab Rotation III — Targeting Microbes in the Digital Ecosystem

UNESCO IOC Project Office for IODE · UN Ocean Decade · MPI-MM · Supervisor: Dr. Pier Luigi Buttigieg
Built a pipeline from scratch to ask a simple question: where does microbial data actually live in the ocean's digital ecosystem? Harvested microbial terms from the SILVA database and Wikipedia (via spaCy natural-language processing), queried the Ocean Data Information System (ODIS) with SPARQL, and mapped the connections between datasets as a directed graph (NetworkX / Gephi) before pinpointing their geographic origins. The results surfaced a societal bias in what gets submitted: plankton studies cluster around eutrophication and fisheries, E. coli data tracks sewage leakage, and the three largest fishing nations — China, Indonesia, India — are barely visible on the map. No universal mandate, no shared vocabulary. Contributed to the specification sheet for the Microbial Essential Ocean Variable under the Global Ocean Observing System (GOOS).

Lab Rotation I — Conservancy of Metabolic Pathways across Microbial Genera

Helmholtz Institute for Functional Marine Biodiversity (HIFMB) · Supervisors: Prof. Dr. A. Murat Eren, Dr. Iva Veseli
Introduced a universal metric for the conservancy of metabolic pathways across microbial genera, built on top of the Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB). Most existing tools overlook pathway dynamics at large scale — so the metric computes weighted arithmetic means of genome completeness and pathway annotation quality, and uses identity scores to distinguish between truly absent and merely undetected pathways, moving beyond conventional threshold-based methods. By weighting enzyme presence and functional significance in genomic context, the approach can surface novel microbial capabilities when applied at scale — demonstrated on the genus Forterrea as a case study.

Feb — Nov 2022

Bachelor Thesis — N₂O Producers vs. Complete Denitrifiers

Eawag · Supervisor: Dr. David Johnson
Studied why N₂O-producing bacteria persist in nature despite competing with complete denitrifiers — which, on first principles, should outcompete them by gaining extra ATP from reducing N₂O to N₂. The question matters climate-wise: nitrous oxide is a potent greenhouse gas and an ozone-depleting one (Ravishankara et al., 2009). Combining anaerobic microbiology, range-expansion experiments, and confocal image analysis, found that both populations exhibit negative frequency dependence — each grows better when rare — letting N₂O producers persist alongside complete denitrifiers. The mechanism appears spatial: non-random self-organisation emerging as the community expands across a surface (Goldschmidt et al., 2021). Spatial and temporal diversity patterns, not just biochemistry, shape which microbial traits dominate — and which climate-relevant processes we can predict or manipulate.
